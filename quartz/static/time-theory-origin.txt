what precisely is the distinction between a time valued money and a labor valued money
Jan 10

This is a conceptual question I can answer directly—no research needed.

Labor theory of value: The value of a commodity derives from the socially necessary labor expended to produce it. This carries embedded assumptions:

    Labor is the source of value (metaphysical claim)
    Effort/toil/exertion matters
    There's often a normative dimension—exploitation occurs when labor isn't compensated proportionally
    Marx distinguished labor time from labor power, which gets complicated

Time theory of value (as you're framing it): Exchange rates converge on time-costs because time is the binding constraint for agents, not because labor is the source of value.

The distinction:

    No metaphysics of value-creation: Time theory doesn't claim time creates value. It just observes that time is the scarce resource being allocated, so prices encode time-costs as information.
    AFK farming matters: Under labor theory, an AFK iron farm "shouldn't" devalue iron (no labor expended). Under time theory, it should—the time-cost dropped, regardless of whether anyone was toiling.
    Subjective value is preserved: Things can still be valued for idiosyncratic reasons. Time theory just says exchange rates converge on time-costs because arbitrage eliminates persistent deviations. You can value a diamond pickaxe for aesthetic reasons, but you can't consistently trade it for more than its time-cost equivalent.
    No exploitation narrative: Labor theory gets you to "surplus value extraction" because labor creates value that capitalists capture. Time theory just says "your hours are fungible across activities, so prices reflect opportunity costs." No villain needed.
    It's descriptive, not prescriptive: Time theory is a claim about what does happen to exchange rates in closed systems, not what should happen or what value really is.

The Minecraft case is clean because you can actually watch exchange rates converge on time-ratios without anyone intending it. It's not that players believe in a time theory of value—it's that the structure of the system forces convergence regardless of beliefs.

so the real question is: would the labor value predict anything different from what the time value one does?

That's the right question. Let me think through this without research first, because it's more of a conceptual exercise.

The theories would diverge in predictions when:

1. Effort-per-unit-time varies

If mining diamonds required intense focus/clicking while mining iron could be done half-asleep, labor theory predicts diamonds should be worth more than pure time-ratio (because more labor per hour). Time theory predicts exchange rates track clock-time regardless of effort intensity.

Testable: Do high-APM activities in games command premiums beyond their time-cost?

2. Automation exists

Labor theory: AFK-farmed goods should retain value (no labor → no value created → supply isn't "real")
Time theory: AFK-farmed goods crash in value (time-cost dropped to near-zero)

Empirically, we see the latter. Iron farms destroy iron's exchange value.

3. Skill differentials

Labor theory (in its "socially necessary" formulation) averages across skill levels—a master craftsman's hour isn't worth more than an apprentice's hour for the same output. Time theory: same prediction, actually. Exchange rates reflect average time-to-acquire.

No divergence here.

4. Risk and variance

A boss with 1% drop rate for a rare item: expected time = 100 kills worth. But the labor experience of getting lucky on kill 3 vs. grinding 500 kills differs enormously.

Labor theory is ambiguous here—does labor count the actual hours you spent, or the expected hours? Time theory clearly predicts prices converge on expected time-cost.

5. Unpleasantness

If an activity is miserable (think: grinding a deeply unfun dungeon), labor theory might predict a premium for the suffering. Time theory predicts no premium—an hour is an hour.

Testable: Do tedious-but-fast activities trade at par with engaging-but-equally-fast ones?

So the main empirical wedge is automation and effort intensity. If AFK farms crash prices and high-APM activities don't command premiums, that's evidence for time over labor.

My guess: time theory wins empirically in virtual economies, which is why you made the distinction in the first place. The Minecraft SMP data presumably shows that how hard you work doesn't matter—only how long.

fascinating. how does marx define capital then, given that the definition from the time value side is so much more clean
Jan 10

Right, because from the time-value side capital is just crystallized time-savings. A diamond pickaxe is capital because it reduces future time-costs. A farm is capital because it generates resources at lower time-cost than manual acquisition. Clean, functional definition—no metaphysics required.

Marx's definition is notoriously more convoluted because he's trying to do several things at once:

    Capital as self-valorizing value: Capital isn't a thing, it's a social relation—value in motion that expands itself. Money becomes capital when it's used to generate more money (M → C → M'). The "thing" (factory, machine) is just the vehicle.
    Constant vs. variable capital: Machines and raw materials are constant capital—they transfer their value to commodities but don't create new value. Only variable capital (labor power) creates surplus value, because workers produce more value than their wages represent.
    Dead labor: Capital is "dead labor, which, vampire-like, lives only by sucking living labor." The machine embodies past labor that now dominates present labor.

The problem is Marx wants capital to be:

    A thing (means of production)
    A process (circulation of value)
    A social relation (class domination)
    A metaphysical claim (only living labor creates value)

...all simultaneously. Which makes the definition slippery.

Under time-theory: capital = anything that reduces future time-costs. That's it. No exploitation narrative required, no distinction between "living" and "dead" labor, no social relations embedded in the definition.

The question is whether Marx's complexity buys you anything explanatory that the simpler definition doesn't. What phenomena does "capital as social relation" predict that "capital as time-savings" misses?

what economic predictions get made based on the time valued thesis?
Jan 10

This is a conceptual question I can think through directly.

If time is the fundamental currency that agents optimize against, and exchange rates converge on time-costs, then:

1. Automation is always deflationary for the automated good

Any process that reduces time-to-acquire should reduce exchange value proportionally. No exceptions for "but someone had to build the machine"—that's a one-time time-cost amortized across all future output.

Prediction: Productivity gains → falling real prices, always. This broadly matches observed reality since the industrial revolution.

2. Prices should converge across goods with equal time-costs

If I can farm wheat or mine iron in the same hour, their exchange rate should approach 1:1 (adjusted for demand fluctuations). Persistent deviations indicate either:

    Measurement error on time-costs
    Barriers to switching activities (specialization lock-in, skills, access)
    Information asymmetry

3. Interest rates are time-preference made explicit

The "natural" interest rate should reflect how much people discount future-time-savings versus present-time-savings. No mysticism about "productivity of capital"—it's just: how much of my time today will I trade for how much of my time tomorrow?

4. Wealth concentration follows from differential time-efficiency

If some agents can convert time → resources more efficiently (better tools, knowledge, location), they accumulate claims on others' future time. Inequality falls out of the model without requiring exploitation—just differential productivity.

5. Post-scarcity = time-cost approaching zero

An economy becomes "post-scarcity" for a good when its time-cost drops below the threshold of attention. This is why late-game Minecraft servers drift toward gift economies—once farms exist, accumulating more stuff costs effectively zero time.

6. Currency selection is predictable

Whatever commodity has the most stable and legible time-cost becomes money. Not the rarest, not the most useful—the most predictable to acquire per unit time.

7. Bubbles are time-cost misestimation

Asset bubbles occur when people systematically underestimate the future time-cost of acquiring something (thinking it will stay scarce when it won't) or overestimate the time-savings it provides.

What the theory doesn't predict well:

    Positional goods (status items whose value is zero-sum)
    Aesthetic preferences (why pay more for beauty?)
    Network effects (value from coordination, not time-savings)

These require bolting on additional mechanisms. The time-value chassis explains exchange rates for producible commodities but not everything humans value.

Austrian roundaboutness as partial predecessor

The closest existing theoretical framework is Böhm-Bawerk's Austrian capital theory from his 1889 Positive Theory of Capital. His concept of "roundaboutness" (Produktionsumwege) treats capital as embodying deferred time—the "sacrifice of Time necessarily involved in the indirect process" of production. More time-intensive, capital-rich production methods yield greater productivity, making capital essentially crystallized waiting. This connects directly to your concept of "capital as crystallized time-savings," though Austrian theory focuses on production time rather than acquisition time.

Ludwig von Mises built on this, arguing that "time too is economized" and that Jevons and Böhm-Bawerk "assigned the element of time its proper place in the system of economic theory." Modern Austrian economists like Roger Garrison (Time and Money) and Peter Lewin have extended this framework into capital-based macroeconomics. Marx's alternative formulation describes capital as "dead labor"—"congelations of labour-time"—which is related but emphasizes labor content rather than pure time.

Time banking literature, pioneered by Teruko Mizushima in Japan (1973) and Edgar Cahn in the United States (1980s), represents the most direct practical application of time as a unit of account. Time banks operate on the principle that "everyone's time is of equal value"—one hour of any service earns one hour of any other service. However, this literature lacks comprehensive theoretical formalization and remains marginal to academic economics.

Conclusion

The specific theoretical synthesis you describe—formalizing time-acquisition cost as the fundamental determinant of exchange value, distinct from both Marxian labor theory and marginalist utility theory—appears to represent genuine unexplored territory in academic economics. The components exist across multiple literatures: Austrian time-capital theory, virtual economy empirical research showing time-price correlations, anthropological gift economy frameworks, and time banking applications. What's missing is the explicit synthesis: a formal theory treating pure time (not labor-time, not subjective preference) as the fundamental unit of value, with predictions about price convergence, scarcity phase transitions, and capital formation.

The virtual economy literature provides an ideal empirical testing ground for such a theory—MMO auction houses offer natural experiments where time-to-acquire is measurable and exchange values are observable.
